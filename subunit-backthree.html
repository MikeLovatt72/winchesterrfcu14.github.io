Back Three

Space exploiters, pendulum unit, last line of defence

← Centres

The Back Three operate as one rotating unit — the pendulum. As the ball shifts one way, the sub-unit repositions automatically: one wing goes wide, the fullback covers centre field, the other wing tucks in as inside cover. When this movement becomes automatic, the Back Three becomes the most dangerous attacking sub-unit on the field.

Primary job: Exploit space. Organise kick receipt. Operate the pendulum. Be the last line of defence and the first option for counter-attack.

The Back Three are where tries are scored and where games are won from nothing. A fullback who organises, wings who hold width, and a sub-unit that rotates automatically will create tries from 70 metres out.

Own 22 — Last line, organise and exit

In the own 22, the Back Three become the most important defensive unit. They receive kicks, cover the back field, and organise the exit. A mistake from the Back Three in their own 22 will almost certainly result in points for the opposition.

  • Fullback: You are the conductor of the back field. Call everything — "mine", "yours left", "kick coming right". Before every scrum and lineout in the own 22, position yourself to receive the anticipated kick. Deep enough to turn it around, close enough to counter if the opportunity arises.
  • Wings: In the own 22, tuck in. Both wings cover the short sides and diagonal kicks. The pendulum — both wings working towards the ball side, fullback covering the other side — must function even under pressure. Call positions constantly.
  • Kick receipt: Call "MARK" loudly the moment you commit to receiving. Two-handed catch, secure the ball to your chest. If you call mark but can't catch cleanly, regather immediately — don't leave the ball on the ground in the own 22.
  • Counter-attack: If the kick is short or the coverage is poor, the Back Three can counter-attack from the own 22. The fullback leads the counter, both wings sprint into wide support channels. First decision: can we get past the gain line? If yes, go. If not, kick long.
Key principle:

The Back Three must be the calmest, most organised unit when defending in the own 22. Noise and urgency from the opposition are designed to force errors. Be composed. Call everything. Trust each other.

Mid-field — The pendulum in action

Mid-field is where the pendulum approach is most visible. The Back Three operates as a rotating unit — as the ball goes one way, the sub-unit repositions to maintain both attacking width and defensive cover simultaneously.

  • The pendulum: As the ball goes right, the right wing goes wide to the touchline. The fullback moves to cover the right side of the field. The left wing tucks in as the inside cover option. When the ball switches left, the whole unit rotates — smoothly and automatically.
  • Width: In attack, the wings provide the widest attacking option. Hold your width — don't drift infield unless you're called for a specific role. If you are infield, you're not creating the overlap the system needs.
  • Kick receipt in mid-field: The fullback organises — "mine if high, yours if low and right". The nearest wing chases every kick the fullback doesn't call. The far wing sprints into support on the counter. React before the ball lands, not after.
  • Counter-attack reads: The fullback must identify the three options before they receive: kick deep, carry into space, or pass to the wing. The decision is made as the ball is in the air, not after it's caught. Hesitation in mid-field costs 20 metres of field position.
Key principle:

The pendulum is automatic — it must require no thinking. Practise the rotation in every session until it feels as natural as breathing. When the pendulum works, the Back Three creates attacking overlaps that no defensive structure can cover.

Opposition 22 — Finish in space

In the opposition's 22, the Back Three are the finishers. All the work done by the forwards and centres to build possession and field position leads here — to the Back Three with space and a chance to score.

  • Wings: Hold your width until the last possible moment. Defenders want you to come infield — don't. If you are wide and flat, a single pass puts you in space with only the try line behind you. Infield wings get tackled in traffic.
  • Fullback: In the opposition 22, join the attacking line from deep. Your angle of approach — wide and deep — makes you invisible to the defence until you're running at pace onto the pass. The fullback running into the line in the opposition 22 is one of the hardest attacking problems to defend.
  • Reading the last defender: Before you receive the ball with the last defender in front of you, read their feet. Are they stepping up (blitz)? Fix them and pass. Are they drifting (drift)? Attack them directly, force the tackle, and offload or present.
  • Support running: Both wings support the ball carrier even in the opposition 22. Trail lines on the outside shoulder. If the ball carrier breaks the last tackle, you must be available for the offload without breaking stride.
Key principle:

Width wins tries. In the opposition 22, the defence has to cover 70 metres of pitch. A Back Three that stays wide, moves fast, and communicates precisely will find the gap that the system creates. Hold width. Sprint onto the pass. Finish.

Defensive responsibilities

The Back Three are the last line of defence. Their defensive responsibilities are specific, technical, and critically important. A breakdown in the Back Three's organisation means the opposition scores.

  • Kick coverage: Fullback calls every kick receipt. Wings sprint into chase positions on every kick. When the opposition kicks, the nearest wing applies pressure to the fullback receiving — even if they can't reach the ball, the pressure forces a hurried clearance. Sprint, don't jog.
  • Choke tackle: When the fullback receives under pressure, the nearest wing sprints to assist the tackle. Two-man choke — wrap the arms, prevent the offload, and slow the momentum. A choke tackle from the wing on the fullback receiving a kick can turn defence into attack.
  • Last man discipline: The wing and fullback who are the last line of defence must never — ever — miss a tackle to try to strip the ball. Your job is to make the tackle first, then contest the ball. A missed tackle from the last defender results in a try.
  • The drift: Outside the 10 and 13, the Back Three drift. Each player marks one man. The fullback covers the final channel. No defender from the Back Three drifts past the touchline — someone must always cover the diagonal kick behind the defensive line.
Key principle:

The last line of defence must be the most organised. Organised communication, organised positioning, organised decision-making. If the Back Three are talking constantly, the opposition can never find space behind the line without the whole unit knowing about it.
