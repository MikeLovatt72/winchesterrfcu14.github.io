Front Five

Engine room, platform builders, set piece foundation

Back Row →

The Front Five are where the game starts. Every piece of attacking play, every defensive structure, every kick — they are all possible because the Front Five won their battle first. Props, hooker and locks are not supporting players. They are the foundation.

Primary job: Win the set piece. Go forward in the carry. Secure every ruck. Provide the go-forward platform from which every other unit operates.

The Front Five sets the tone. If the scrum goes forward and the lineout is clean, the backs have freedom. If the set piece is shaky, everyone is under pressure.

Roles by zone

Select a zone to see your specific responsibilities. Understanding what your sub-unit does in each zone is as important as knowing your individual skills.

Own 22 — Protect and escape

The Front Five's primary job in the own 22 is to give the backs a clean platform to kick or carry out of pressure. This means winning the scrum and winning the lineout cleanly, every time. There is no experimentation in the 22 — execution only.

  • Scrum: Drive straight, stay low, front row hips below shoulder height. The 8-man unit drives together — any prop who comes up early kills the drive for the whole pack.
  • Lineout: Quick, clean ball to the catcher. Caller controls the throw — simple calls, low risk. Hooker throws to the safe zone (middle or front). No clever plays in the 22.
  • Ruck: Two bodies maximum at the ruck in our own 22. The other three must reset into defensive positions. Don't commit the whole pack to a ruck 5m from our own try line.
  • Communication: Front five must talk — "same", "straight", "drive" — constant communication. The backs cannot see what's happening at the scrum. We tell them.
Key principle:

Security over ambition. Win the set piece. Give the 9 clean ball. Let the backs carry us out of trouble. Our job is to make their job possible.

Mid-field — Build platform, go forward

This is where the game is won or lost up front. Mid-field scrums and lineouts should be where the Front Five are at their most assertive. More space to work with, defenders further from the try line — this is attack territory for the pack.

  • Scrum: Drive with intent. The #8 pick-and-go from the base is a genuine weapon here — call it in the huddle, execute together. Props must stay bound until the No.8 is clear of the scrum.
  • Lineout: Variation available — front, middle and back calls. Locks should compete for the ball, not just catch it. Hooker: the throw goes where the caller says, not where it's comfortable.
  • Carrying: In mid-field, locks and props carry on the short side and at second receiver. Body position is everything — low, driving through the contact, presenting the ball away from the body.
  • Ruck: First body always secures. Second body drives through. Third body asks: is my arrival making this better or cluttering the ruck? Only enter if you improve the picture.
Key principle:

Go forward. The Front Five sets the tone — if we win the collision in mid-field, the backs have a running start. If we go backwards, the defence resets and the advantage is lost.

Opposition 22 — Finish the job

In the opposition's 22, the Front Five must be the most dangerous unit on the field. Territory has been won — now the pack finishes the move. Set piece in the opposition 22 is a direct scoring opportunity.

  • Scrum: Drive with maximum intensity. 8-man shove. If the opposition is under pressure, call the #8 to pick and crash — one pass to the try line from here. Decision made before the scrum, not during it.
  • Lineout: Maul option available here. Clean catch, immediate maul formation, drive for the try line. Props bind the jumper down and drive — chest on the jumper's back, hips driving forward. Don't stop until you're across the line or the referee calls it.
  • Carrying: Short carries close to the ruck, breaking the gain line each phase. Force the defenders to commit numbers to the ruck — that opens the edge for the backs.
  • Patience: Don't try to force the try in one hit. Work through phases, control the ball, keep the defence under pressure. The try will come. Rushing the finish means giving the ball away in the 22.
Key principle:

Controlled aggression. We have worked hard to get here. The Front Five doesn't hurry in the opposition 22 — they impose themselves, phase by phase, until the space opens.

Defensive responsibilities

The Front Five's defensive role extends well beyond the tackle. At U14, the ability of the Front Five to compete at the breakdown, slow opposition ball, and recover into defensive lines is a significant competitive advantage.

  • Guard and post: After every ruck, one prop or flanker covers post (directly behind the #9) and one covers guard (one channel wider). This prevents the opposition #8 or blind-side runner from going around the corner untouched.
  • Scrum defence: When the opposition has the scrum, front row must stay bound until the ball is clear. Flankers peel immediately to guard/post. Locks support the bind or split to cover channels. Do not rush off the scrum and leave the front row isolated.
  • Lineout defence: Contested lineouts begin at U14 — locks must compete for throws. Even disrupting the opposition's clean catch forces them to adjust their moves. Hooker: watch the opposition's throw pattern across the first two lineouts of the game.
  • Tackling: Props and locks tackle low on the carry. Technique: close the gap, drop the shoulder, head to the side, wrap both arms, drive through the hips. A bounced tackle in the opposition 22 can cost three points.
Key principle:

Defence as a weapon. A dominant, well-organised Front Five in defence demoralises opposition packs. When they stop going forward, their whole team loses confidence.
