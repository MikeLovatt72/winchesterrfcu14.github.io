Back Row

Connectors, disruptors, pod system leaders

← Front Five Half Backs →

The Back Row are the most versatile unit on the field. They carry, tackle, compete at the breakdown, chase kicks, and cover the back field. The back row player who only does one of these things well will always be found wanting. At U14, the Back Row is where the game's physical battles are decided.

Primary job: Lead the forward pod in attack. Compete at every breakdown in defence. Cover the fringes. Bridge the Front Five and the backs.

No.8 is the apex predator — powerful, decisive, and the link between the set piece and open play. Flankers are the game's most relentless workers.

Roles by zone

Select a zone to see your specific responsibilities. Understanding what your sub-unit does in each zone is as important as knowing your individual skills.

Own 22 — Restrict and clear

In the own 22, the Back Row's job is to eliminate risk and create the exit. This means covering every short-side threat, winning every breakdown, and being the unit that physically prevents the opposition from scoring from close range.

  • No.8: Cover the base of every ruck — guard the #9 and prevent any opposition snipe. You are the last line of defensive organisation for the forward pod.
  • Flankers: Blindside covers post (inside shoulder of the ruck). Openside looks to compete — time the jackal precisely. In the 22, a successful turnover can immediately relieve pressure.
  • Pick and go: Avoid unless the play is clear. In the 22, a spilled ball or slow pick means the opposition has a five-metre scrum. Keep it simple.
  • Communication: Back Row must talk to the front five at every ruck. "Seal left", "coming through right", "jackaling" — the Front Five needs to know what the Back Row are doing so they can drive accordingly.
Key principle:

In the own 22, the Back Row is the defensive engine. No risks, no fancy plays. Win the ruck, give the 9 clean ball, exit the 22.

Mid-field — Lead the attack

Mid-field is where the Back Row comes alive. The pod system — No.8 leading with the flankers in support — is the primary attacking weapon from mid-field set piece and phase play.

  • The pod: No.8 leads, flankers on either shoulder 2-3m back. No.8 takes the carry and presents. Flankers arrive — one secures the ruck, one resets to become the next carrier. The pod cycles — you carry, you present, you reset, you carry again.
  • Reading the picture: Before each carry, the No.8 asks: is there a gap short-side? Is the defence flat or deep? Is the 10 available for an offload? Make the decision before you receive the ball.
  • Openside flanker: Your job in mid-field is to arrive at every breakdown before the opposition. Not to carry — to compete and win back the ball or slow their ball. Your speed to the breakdown is your most important attribute.
  • Blindside flanker: Secondary carry option. Hold depth off the No.8 — if the No.8 draws two defenders, you go straight. Read the picture in real time.
Key principle:

The Back Row is the engine of the 1-3-3-1. In mid-field, you drive the system forward. Every carry that goes beyond the gain line creates the opportunity for the backs to exploit the space you've created.

Opposition 22 — Impose and finish

In the opposition's 22, the Back Row is the most dangerous unit. Close-range carries, maul support, and breakdown compete — these are the tools that score tries or create the conditions for others to score.

  • No.8 pick-and-go: The most direct route to the try line in the opposition 22 is the No.8 picking from the base of the scrum and driving. Work this with the #9 before the scrum — the decision is made before the ball comes in, not after.
  • Maul support: If the lineout maul is called, the Back Row are the driving engine. Both flankers bind immediately behind the jumper — chest on back, shoulder driving forward, feet pushing. Drive until the referee's whistle or the try line.
  • Phase play: Short, brutal carries between the posts. Draw defenders in. When the defence collapses inward on the Back Row pod, that's the moment the 9 goes wide to the backs. Timing is everything — don't release the ball early.
  • Jackal: After the tackle, the openside flanker times a legal jackal attempt. In the opposition 22, the opposition are under pressure and ball presentation may be rushed — this is when jackaling is most effective.
Key principle:

Apex predators. In the opposition 22, the Back Row must have the mindset that they will score. Not might — will. That belief drives the physicality required to finish.

Defensive responsibilities

The Back Row is the most versatile defensive unit. You cover the fringes, compete at the breakdown, cover kicks, and fill gaps in the defensive line. No other unit covers as much defensive ground.

  • Fringe defence: No.8 and blindside cover the channels immediately outside the ruck. If the #9 snipes, one of you must be there. If the opposition goes short-side, one of you must be there. You cannot both commit to the ruck.
  • Jackaling: Openside flanker — your primary defensive role is competing at the breakdown. Body position: low, over the ball, feet behind the ball, bind legally. If you can't secure a penalty, at least slow their ball. Never enter a ruck you cannot win legally.
  • Drift coverage: When the backs defend, the Back Row covers the crash ball. If the opposition's #12 hits up directly, a Back Row player must be available in the defensive line outside the #10.
  • Kick coverage: No.8 and blindside cover kick receipts in mid-field when the fullback is otherwise engaged. Know where the fullback is — if they've gone into the line, you are the last defensive cover for kick receipts.
Key principle:

Cover the fringes. The Back Row is the unit that ensures nothing goes unpunished around the edges of the defensive structure. Be everywhere.
