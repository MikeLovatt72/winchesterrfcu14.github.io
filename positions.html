1·3
Props
Front Five · Loosehead & Tighthead

The props are the engine of the scrum and the anchors of the lineout. At U14 the scrum becomes a genuine contest — your technique, your binding, and your ability to stay low under pressure will determine whether the team goes forward or backwards from every set piece.

Good
  • Engages correctly in Crouch-Bind-Set without prompting
  • Maintains body position (hips below shoulders) for the duration of the scrum
  • Completes tackles safely — head to the side, wraps both arms
  • Arrives at rucks consistently and secures the ball
  • Understands their defensive position after every set piece
Excellent
  • Actively drives the scrum — generating power through the hips, not the back
  • Reacts to scrum commands (tightening bind, adjusting angle) without instruction
  • Carries effectively at close range — presenting cleanly after contact
  • Reaches rucks before the opposition and drives through on arrival
  • Understands and executes guard/post responsibility at every breakdown
  • Communicates with the hooker on scrum binding adjustments mid-scrum
Outstanding
  • Consistently wins individual scrum battles — puts opposition props under legal pressure
  • Mobile carrier across the width of the field — not just at the fringes
  • Communicates front row adjustments independently to the team
  • Arrives at breakdowns faster than opposition counterparts — changes the dynamic
  • Understands scrum strategy — when to wheel, when to hold, when to call the pick
  • Demonstrates outstanding behaviours — leads by example in every session
2
Hooker
Front Five · Lineout thrower

The hooker has the most technically demanding role in the team. Lineout throwing is a skill that requires hours of solitary practice to develop. Combined with scrum technique and the mobility demands of a modern hooker, this is one of the hardest positions on the field to play well at U14.

Good
  • Throws straight to the target zone consistently in practice
  • Engages correctly in the scrum front row — binds, hooks, drives
  • Tackles safely and effectively
  • Arrives at rucks and contributes to securing the ball
Excellent
  • Lineout throwing is consistent under match pressure — not just in drills
  • Can accurately throw to front, middle, and back under instruction from the caller
  • Carries effectively as a ball-running option around the fringes
  • Communicates with the lineout caller during the lineout — reads the opposition's positioning and adjusts
  • Mobile across the field — gets to breakdowns in open play
Outstanding
  • Lineout throw is the team's weapon — the opposition cannot predict where the ball is going
  • Adapts throw length and trajectory to the opposition's lineout defence
  • Genuine carrying threat — defenders must account for the hooker as a runner
  • Leads the front row communication independently
  • Practises throwing independently outside of team sessions — 20 minutes per day
4·5
Locks
Front Five · Lineout specialists

The locks are the heartbeat of the pack. In the scrum they provide the primary drive. In the lineout they are the primary jumpers. In open play they are the relentless workers — carrying, rucking, covering. The best locks at U14 are those who make every rep count in every session.

Good
  • Binds correctly in the 8-man scrum — both arms through, driving straight
  • Presents as a lineout jumper — two-footed jump, hands high and early
  • Carries effectively at close range
  • Works consistently at rucks — arrives and drives
Excellent
  • Wins own lineout ball cleanly under opposition pressure
  • Understands lineout calls — adjusts jumping position based on the call without instruction
  • Generates genuine scrum power — able to drive the opposition backwards
  • Carries in the mid-field as a second-phase option, not just close to the ruck
  • Contests opposition lineout throws — reads the throw and competes legally
Outstanding
  • The team's primary lineout platform — their performance defines how many lineouts we win
  • Provides leadership of the lineout unit — communicates adjustments to the hooker mid-game
  • Physical presence in carrying — defenders cannot simply absorb their carry
  • Engine room of the scrum — consistently wins individual scrum contests
  • Mobility that surprises — reaches breakdowns in the backs before opposition players
  • Sets the standards for effort and attitude in the forward unit
6·7
Flankers
Back Row · Blindside & Openside

Flankers cover more ground than any other position. They must be at every breakdown, every set piece, and every defensive line. The openside flanker's speed to the breakdown is the most critical individual athletic attribute in the team. The blindside flanker's ability to carry effectively and cover short-side threats is equally important.

Good
  • Arrives at rucks consistently and contributes to securing the ball
  • Tackles effectively — correct technique, both arms, head to the side
  • Peels correctly from lineout and scrum to cover guard/post
  • Carries in the pod formation — works off the No.8
Excellent
  • Openside: consistently the first player to arrive at opposition breakdowns — slowing or winning their ball
  • Blindside: genuine carrying threat who defenders must account for in their planning
  • Jackals legally and effectively — correct body position, both feet behind the ball
  • Covers guard/post without instruction every single ruck — automatic habit
  • Works in synchrony with the No.8 — communicates roles before each sequence
Outstanding
  • Changes the game through breakdown dominance — wins penalties, wins turnovers, slows ball consistently
  • Carries across the full width of the field — not just in the pod
  • Makes the No.8's job easier — covers their defensive position when the 8 carries
  • Reads the game a phase ahead — anticipates where the breakdown will be before the tackle
  • Communication leader of the forward unit — sets the pace and clarity of information
8
Number 8
Back Row · Pod leader · Apex predator

The No.8 is the most powerful tool the forward pack has in attack. They can pick up from the base of the scrum — something not possible at U13 — and they lead the pod in every phase of play. The best No.8s at U14 combine physical power with intelligent decision-making: knowing when to go themselves and when to release the flankers.

Good
  • Understands the pick-and-go from the scrum base — executes it cleanly when called
  • Leads the pod — receives the ball at the front of the pod formation
  • Tackles effectively and consistently
  • Covers guard/post at every ruck without instruction
Excellent
  • Pick-and-go from the scrum is a genuine weapon — executed at pace and with correct presentation
  • Reads the defensive picture before calling the pod play — communicates to the 9 whether to pass, pick, or hold
  • Carries beyond the gain line consistently — not just to the gain line
  • Works fluently with both flankers — the three operate as one unit
  • Covers the back field for kick receipt when the fullback enters the line
Outstanding
  • Dominant physical presence — defenders must double-commit to stop the No.8 carry
  • Calls the pod plays themselves — independently directing the forward pod
  • Decision to pick, pass or hold is consistently the correct one — changes the game
  • Works in close partnership with the 9 — the scrum base combination is the team's primary attacking threat
  • Sets the physical standard for the entire team — leads by example in every contact situation
  • Genuine apex predator. In the opposition 22 the entire defence must plan around stopping the No.8.
9
Scrum-half
Half Backs · Base of the diamond

The scrum-half is the most vocal player on the field. Every ruck, every lineout, every scrum — the 9 is talking. Setting the tempo, communicating the play, directing the pod. At U14, the scrum-half can box kick for the first time — making them one of the most tactically influential players in the team.

Good
  • Passes cleanly off both hands from the ruck base at speed
  • Communicates play calls to the forward pod before every ruck
  • Box kick technique is developing — getting height and distance
  • Understands defensive positioning after every set piece
Excellent
  • Pass is consistently accurate under pressure — still clean when fatigued
  • Box kick is a weapon — lands in the target zone more often than not
  • Communicates play calls before the ball leaves the previous ruck — not when it arrives
  • Reads when to go themselves versus when to pass — decision is consistently correct
  • Organises the defensive line around every ruck — "blitz", "drift", "guard off"
Outstanding
  • The base of the diamond functions because of their communication — everyone knows the play before the ball arrives
  • Box kick is the team's primary exit weapon — consistent flight, height and landing zone
  • Controls the tempo of the game — speeds up when the defence is disorganised, slows down when the team needs to reset
  • Their snipe from the base is a genuine try-scoring threat that defenders must plan for
  • Vocal, precise and early in all communication — the team's conductor
  • Can be the most influential player on the field. Actively is, in most games.
10
Fly-half
Half Backs · Pivot of the diamond

The fly-half is the pivot of the 1-3-3-1. They receive from the 9, read the defensive picture, and make the play. At U14, the full kicking portfolio is available — kick, pass, or carry. The best fly-halves are those who make the right choice, not the impressive choice.

Good
  • Passes accurately to both centres at varying depths
  • Makes a genuine kicking, passing, or carrying threat — defenders must account for all three
  • Calls the defensive line — blitz or drift — on most occasions
  • Understands zone-based kicking decisions (own 22 = kick long, opposition 22 = keep ball)
Excellent
  • Decision before ball receipt — knows what they will do before the pass arrives
  • Kicking game is reliable — box kick, cross-field, and grubber all in their toolkit
  • Running game fixes defenders — their threat draws coverage and creates space for others
  • Defensive calls are early, clear and consistently correct
  • Works with the 9 as a genuine partnership — both know what the other is about to do
Outstanding
  • Game management — ability to read what the team needs (go fast, slow down, kick, spread) and execute it
  • Kicking game is a weapon — consistently creates pressure with kick placement, not just distance
  • Running game changes the game — two or more line breaks per match
  • Defensive organisation makes the whole back line more effective
  • Starter plays are called and executed with precision — the set piece is an attacking weapon under their direction
  • Reads the game a phase ahead — communicates to the team before the picture develops
11·14
Wings
Back Three · Pendulum unit

The wings are the space exploiters. At U14, with a full-size pitch, there is more space on the edges than any defender can cover — but only if the wings hold their width and sprint onto the ball. The wing who drifts infield is the wing who never gets the ball in space.

Good
  • Holds width — doesn't drift infield unless specifically called
  • Catches high balls — two hands, calls "mine", secures cleanly
  • Chases every kick — arrives on the receiver with pressure
  • Understands the pendulum — moves with the ball direction
Excellent
  • Consistently the widest player — creates the overlap that the 13 exploits
  • Kick receipt under match pressure — catches cleanly, makes the correct decision immediately
  • Counter-attack from kick receipt — reads when to run, when to kick back
  • Tucks in correctly on the blind side when the ball goes the other way — pendulum is automatic
  • Trail lines in attack — arrives in support of the ball carrier
Outstanding
  • Finishing is the team's primary scoring weapon — defenders cannot match their pace with the ball in space
  • Counter-attack changes games — reads the defensive picture instantly and exploits it from deep
  • Kick chase is one of the best in the team — consistently converts into turnovers or territorial gains
  • Communication with the fullback and other wing is constant and effective — the pendulum never breaks down
  • Brings the ball into contact in the right position when carries are necessary
12
Inside Centre
Centres · First receiver

The inside centre is the first receiver from the fly-half. They must be running flat and fast onto the ball — a standing 12 is the easiest defensive read in the game. Their job is to fix the inside defender and create the overlap, or carry hard and commit two defenders to the ruck.

Good
  • Runs onto the ball — doesn't catch it standing still
  • Carries effectively — reaches the gain line and presents cleanly
  • Covers the inside defensive channel — doesn't drift wide too early
  • Communicates with the 13 on defensive positioning
Excellent
  • Running line at pace forces the defence to commit — creates the 13's attacking space
  • Reads whether to carry or pass before the ball arrives — decision is consistent
  • Line shooter option — can step up to hit the ball flat when the defence is drifting too early
  • Defensive line speed — steps up aggressively on the snap of every ruck
  • Covers the inside shoulder of the opposition 10 in defence — never drifts off this responsibility
Outstanding
  • Midfield penetration — carries through the gain line to the second defender, forcing the defence to commit numbers
  • Instinctive offload — finds the 13 or the back row in support without looking
  • Defensive communication — sets the pace of the defensive line for the whole back line
  • The go-to player when the team needs forward momentum in mid-field — consistently delivers it
  • Leads by example in every defensive situation — communicates to the 13 before every defensive ruck
13
Outside Centre
Centres · Space reader & edge exploiter

The outside centre is the player who turns the 12's work into tries. When the 12 has fixed the inside defender, the 13 exploits the space. Their reading of the last defender — blitz or hold — is the most important individual decision in the back line at the moment of attack.

Good
  • Receives from the 12 at pace — doesn't slow down to catch the ball
  • Attacks the space outside the last inside defender
  • Communicates with the right wing on defensive positioning
  • Tackles effectively in the outside channel
Excellent
  • Reads the last defender accurately — fixes them and delivers a try-scoring pass, or goes through them
  • Scissors play with the 12 is reliable — both players execute without confusion
  • Communicates the wing's defensive position in real time
  • Carries beyond the gain line and presents accurately for the next phase
  • Defensive drift is disciplined — marks one man and doesn't overrun into the wing's territory
Outstanding
  • Every time the 13 receives the ball, the defence is under immediate pressure — pace, decision-making, and execution are all elite
  • Creates tries for the wing from situations where most players would be stopped
  • Defensive organisation of the outside back line — their communication makes the wing and fullback more effective
  • Counter-attacking threat from deep — joins the back three in kick receipt situations
  • Breaks the gain line carrying and simultaneously finds the support — carries are tries in waiting
15
Full-back
Back Three · Organiser of the pendulum

The full-back is the team's last line of defence and first line of counter-attack. They must be the calmest, most organised player on the field under a high ball. At U14, with a full-size pitch, the full-back must cover more ground than at any previous age group. Their ability to read the game from deep is the quality that separates good full-backs from excellent ones.

Good
  • Catches high balls reliably — calls "mine", two hands, secures to chest
  • Kicks effectively under pressure — gets the ball into the opposition's half
  • Covers the back field — is in the right position when the opposition kicks
  • Communicates with the wings on kick receipts
Excellent
  • High ball is a strength — takes balls other players would drop or avoid
  • Counter-attack decision is consistently correct — knows when to run and when to kick back
  • Joins the attacking line from deep — their angle and timing fixes defenders and creates overlaps
  • Organises the pendulum — both wings move based on their calls
  • Kick under pressure is accurate — doesn't just clear to safety, clears to territory
Outstanding
  • The back three functions as a unit because of their organisation — the pendulum is automatic
  • Counter-attack changes games — reads the defensive structure from 50 metres out and exploits the gaps
  • Joining the line from deep is the team's most unpredictable attacking option — creates a guaranteed overlap when timed correctly
  • Kicking game from deep is one of the team's primary weapons — accurate, varied and always with a chasing structure behind it
  • Never caught out of position — the back field is always covered regardless of what the other 14 players are doing
  • Loudest voice in the back field — their communication makes every other back three player more confident
