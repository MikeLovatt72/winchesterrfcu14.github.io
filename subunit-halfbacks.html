Half Backs

Decision-makers, directors, tempo setters

← Back Row Centres →

The Half Backs are the game's conductors. Every piece of play runs through them. The 9 is the base of the diamond — the fulcrum between the forwards and the backs. The 10 is the pivot — reading the picture and choosing the play. Their partnership is the most important relationship on the field.

Primary job: Control the tempo. Direct the attack. Organise the defence. Make the right decision before the opposition makes a mistake.

Can be the most influential players on the field. A half-back partnership that communicates early, decides before receiving the ball, and manages tempo will change game outcomes.

Own 22 — Control and exit

In the own 22, the Half Backs control the exit. Simple decisions, clean execution. Every poor decision here can result in three or seven points for the opposition.

  • Scrum-half: Move the ball quickly and accurately. First option from your own 22 is always the kick — long, high, to the corners. If the kick is not on, pass to the 10 and let them decide. Do not attempt a pick and run from your own 22 unless the gap is enormous.
  • Fly-half: Stand deeper than normal to give yourself time on the ball. The primary exit option in the own 22 is a line kick to touch — high, angled, into the corners of the opposition half. Get it out of play. A box kick in your own 22 is high-risk: it stays in the field of play and gives the opposition a 50/50 contest close to your try line. Use the box kick in mid-field, not here.
  • The exception: If the kick receiver sees space in the opposition backfield and is confident in their kick chase game, a well-struck kick into that space can turn defence into attack. The key word is confident — if there is any doubt, the ball goes to touch.
  • Communication: 9 and 10 agree on the play before it happens. In the own 22 the default call is always: "kick to touch". Any variation on that default must be explicitly called — "chip behind right", "carry to 12", "wide ball left". Eliminate on-the-fly decision-making by making the decision first.
  • No heroics: Nothing clever in the own 22. Set piece → kick or carry to safety → exit. The bravest thing the 9 and 10 can do in their own 22 is choose the simple option.
Key principle:

The 9 and 10 are the calmest players in the own 22. Noise, pressure, and urgency are the opposition's weapons. Control the tempo. Breathe. Make the right call.

Mid-field — Direct the game

Mid-field is where the Half Backs make their most important contribution. This is where the game is played — and where the 9 and 10 direct every aspect of the attack.

  • Scrum-half: Vocal. Very vocal. Talking all the time. Every ruck — "coming left", "backs right", "box kick". Every lineout — "going short", "going long". Every scrum — "#8 pick", "pass to 10". You set the tempo of the entire team. If you are silent, the team is directionless.
  • Fly-half: You are the pivot of the diamond. In mid-field you have three decisions: kick to create a contest, pass to attack the backs, or run to fix a defender. Make the decision based on what the defensive line is giving you — not what you want to do.
  • Reading the defence: Before you receive the ball, scan the defensive line. Is it flat or deep? How many defenders are covering the backs? Is the No.6 blitzing off the ruck? These questions must be answered before the ball arrives.
  • Tempo management: The 9 controls the speed of the game from the ruck base. Fast ball when we have the advantage; slow down when we need to reorganise or are under pressure. The 10 must communicate to the 9 whether they want fast or slow ball — never assume.
Key principle:

Can be the most influential player on the field. The 9 and 10 who communicate clearly, make decisions early, and control tempo will change the outcome of games entirely independently of the score.

Opposition 22 — Attack with precision

In the opposition 22, the Half Backs shift from directors to attackers. The work done in mid-field to create field position means nothing if the Half Backs can't convert it into points.

  • No kicking: The kicking game is turned off in the opposition 22 — you are surrendering territory you've already won. Keep the ball in hand. Attack the space.
  • Scrum-half: Quick, flat passes to the 10 at pace. If the opportunity arises, a 9 snipe from the base of the ruck in the opposition 22 can be devastating — but only if the gap is genuinely there. Don't force it.
  • Fly-half: Attack the gain line. Get your feet moving forward before you receive the ball — a running 10 in the opposition 22 fixes at least one defender and creates the overlap or the space for the carry. The standing 10 is much easier to defend.
  • Starter plays: Pre-agreed set-piece moves from scrums and lineouts must be executed with precision here. The decision is made in the huddle, not on the pitch. Both 9 and 10 know exactly what they're doing before the ball comes in.
Key principle:

Patience creates the opening. The Half Backs in the opposition 22 must trust the system — keep building through phases, keep the defenders moving, and the try will come from the overload rather than the forced pass.

Defensive responsibilities

The 9 and 10 are the organisers of the entire defensive line. When they are communicating effectively, the defence functions. When they are silent, defenders are guessing.

  • Scrum-half: After the ball leaves the ruck, immediately read the picture and call the play. "Blitz", "drift", "guard off" — these calls must come before the opposition's 10 has made their decision. You are reacting to the ruck base, not the backs. Be first.
  • Fly-half: You call the defensive line. "Up", "hold", "blitz", "drift" — one clear call, communicated to both sides of the line. The whole back line responds to the 10's call. If you hesitate, the line hesitates.
  • Blitz call: Used when the opposition 9 is slow and their 10 is standing flat. All backs step up aggressively on the snap. The 10 calls "BLITZ" as the 9 touches the ball. Front foot tackle, not a drift. Never blitz against a deep 10 with a fast pass — you'll be beaten in the air.
  • Drift call: Used in open field. Each back drifts one man out. 10 marks the opposition 10 or 12 — never letting them get outside you. Never drift past the last man — someone always covers kick returns.
Key principle:

Defensive organisation wins games. A half-back partnership that calls every ruck, every line and every blitz will make every other defensive player's job easier. Communication is the tackle.
