Centres

Midfield engine, defensive line leaders, space creators

← Half Backs Back Three →

The Centres are the physical and tactical engine of the back line. They absorb defensive pressure, create attacking overlaps, and lead the defensive line speed. The partnership between 12 and 13 must function as one — communicating, supporting, and responding to each other automatically.

Primary job: Create and exploit space in attack. Lead defensive line speed. Fix defenders to create overlaps for the Back Three. Communicate constantly.

The 12 is the crash point and the threat — a running 12 in the right channel demands a response from the defence. The 13 is the space reader — turning the defensive response into an attacking opportunity.

Own 22 — Depth and cover

In the own 22, the Centres must provide depth behind the carry and cover for defensive kicks. Flat-line centre play in the own 22 is dangerous — you need space to read and react.

  • Positioning: Stand deeper than normal in the own 22. Give yourself time to assess kicks and short-side plays. The opposition will look to use the short side — 12 must be ready to cover it immediately.
  • Communication: Talk to the 10 constantly. If the 10 is going to kick, the 12 must be ready to chase or cover. If the 10 is carrying, the 12 takes their cue to support or drift wide to 13.
  • No cross-field runs: In the own 22, avoid running across the defensive line. If you're taken out of the picture by going the wrong way, the gap you leave can be exploited for a try.
  • Carrying out: If asked to carry in your own 22, the priority is simple — get past the gain line and present cleanly. No offloads, no speculative plays. Ball security above everything.
Key principle:

Stay big in the own 22. Cover the channels, communicate clearly, and don't try anything clever. The Centres' discipline in the own 22 is the foundation of the team's ability to exit.

Mid-field — Midfield penetration

Mid-field is where the Centres earn their selection. This is the engine room of the back line — the area where penetration, line breaks, and defensive pressure are all created and absorbed.

  • 12 — Inside Centre: First receiver off the 10. You must be running onto the ball — not standing and catching. A flat, fast 12 forces the defence to make a rapid decision. The inside shoulder is the threat; the outside pass is the release valve. Read the outside centre's positioning before you receive the ball.
  • 13 — Outside Centre: You are the space exploiter. If the 12 has drawn in the last inside defender, you attack the space immediately outside them. If the defender drifts out to you, you fix them and pass early to the wing. Never carry into a defensive overlap — find the pass.
  • Line shooter: A pre-agreed play where the inside centre steps up early to hit the ball at pace, targeting the opposition's drift defender before they can set their feet. Most effective in mid-field when the opposition is drifting out too quickly.
  • Support lines: Both centres must provide support lines on either side of every ball carrier. If the 8 carries, the 12 is on his outside shoulder. If the 13 carries, the 12 is looping to their outside. You should never be standing watching — always moving to support.
Key principle:

The Centres must be the most dangerous decision-making unit in the team. Every time either centre receives the ball, the defence should not know whether you will carry, pass, or kick. Unpredictability is your greatest weapon.

Opposition 22 — Create the try

In the opposition 22, the Centres become the primary playmakers. They create the overlap, fix the last defenders, and put the wingers and fullback into space. Execution under pressure here is the difference between scoring and not scoring.

  • 12: Your job in the opposition 22 is to attack the inside shoulder of the opposition's last defensive centre. If you can get your shoulder past theirs, the referee will see a legitimate tackle attempt that creates a ruck inside the 22 — that's repeat phase ball in the perfect position.
  • 13: In the opposition 22, you face the last line of defence. Read whether the last defender is going to blitz (step up aggressively) or hold (wait for you to commit). If they blitz, fix them and throw a long flat pass to the wing. If they hold, attack them directly and offload.
  • Scissors play: The 12 runs a scissors line with the 13 — both running in opposite directions past each other. One of them will be on the correct side of the last defender. Practise this until it's automatic.
  • Patience: Don't force the try from 20 metres. Keep working through phases — each carry should advance 3-5 metres and draw a defender into the ruck. When the last defenders are isolated, the space is there.
Key principle:

Create, don't score. The Centres' job in the opposition 22 is usually to create the try for someone else — the wing, the fullback, the 10 on a run. Ego has no place here. The assist is as important as the try.

Defensive responsibilities

The Centres are the leaders of the defensive back line. When the Centres are communicating and moving up aggressively, the whole defensive structure functions. When they are passive or silent, the opposition's backs will run through mid-field at will.

  • Line speed: Both centres must step up on the snap — not after the pass. The moment the #9 touches the ball, the defensive line moves forward. A flat, fast defensive line makes the opposition's passes difficult and their carries dangerous.
  • Inside shoulder coverage: 12 must always cover the inside shoulder of the opposition 10 or 12. Never drift wide and leave the crashing inside runner undefended. That's the most common defensive error at this age group — the inside centre drifts out and the 10 or 12 goes through the gap.
  • Communication: 13 must talk to the wings constantly. "Drift", "hold", "yours outside" — the wingers need to know whether the last outside centre is covering their inside shoulder or drifting to them. Silence creates overlaps.
  • Kick coverage: Both centres must be aware of kick threats from the opposition 10. If the 10 looks like they're kicking, the nearer centre immediately becomes a chaser — sprint alongside the kick trajectory and put pressure on the receiver.
Key principle:

Defensive line speed as a weapon. When the Centres step up aggressively together, they take time and space away from the opposition's most dangerous decision-making phase. Fast, flat, communicating Centres win defensive battles.
