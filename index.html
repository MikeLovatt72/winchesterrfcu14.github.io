Programme Introduction

Welcome to the Summer of Fun

As we look ahead to the U14s season, we've pulled together this resource to help parents and players understand the fundamentals of how to train, condition and prepare. This is a partner piece to the Transition to U14s document, which lays out the key rule differences between U13s and U14s.

Those differences will, by necessity, drive a more structured approach to how we prepare, coach and execute the U14s season. Essentially the U14s season is a transition year — a deliberate "by design" RFU pathway, bridging the step up from children's rugby to the junior game. The laws, pitch size and team shape all move closer to the adult version of the game, by design.

What this means for players

Structure

Players will start to learn true positional roles. Sub-units grow in importance, creating the foundation for the 15-player team.

Space

More space to manage and take advantage of while adjusting to the 15-player game.

Set piece

Scrums, lineouts and mauls grow in importance and start to be used as attacking weapons and platforms to launch plays.

Dimensions

More options on both sides of the ball — kicking in all aspects, and the addition of flankers.

Strategy

Understanding different zones on the pitch and what we are trying to achieve in those zones — the foundation of our 1-3-3-1 system.

With our progression to an older age group we will look to incorporate approaches and techniques to support player-led goals, player-led consistency, and team developmental goals both on and off the field.

— Martin & the U14 Coaching Team

Our four aims for the season

1
Game Understanding

Reading the game — shape in attack and defence, knowing where to be, making correct decisions under pressure. What's my next job?

2
Outstanding Behaviours

Attitude, coachability, communication on the pitch, effort, and how a player carries themselves in a team environment.

3
Athletic Development

Speed, endurance, power, agility, and movement quality. Not just current fitness — potential to develop physically through consistent work.

4
Fundamental Skills

Catch & Pass, Carry, Tackle, and Breakdown — the four core skills coached and observed at every session.

Our playing approach

The 1-3-3-1 System & Diamond Formation

This season Winchester RFC U14s play a structured 1-3-3-1 system, executed through the diamond formation. This is not just a shape — it is a philosophy about how we use space, how our sub-units interact, and how every player understands their role in both attack and defence.

What is the 1-3-3-1?

The 1-3-3-1 is a way of structuring the team around four zones — with one player at the base (typically the #9), three forwards as a tight attacking pod, three backs as a wide attacking line, and one player at the apex (typically the fullback or #10 as a pivot). The diamond formation is the shape this creates across the width of the pitch.

At its core, the system is about creating and attacking space intelligently. Rather than running in straight lines or playing random offloads, every player has a defined starting position, a primary job, and a support responsibility.

Why this system?

At U14 the pitch becomes full-size. There is far more space — but only if you have the structure to find it and use it. The 1-3-3-1 gives players a framework to make decisions quickly and correctly: where should I be, what does the picture tell me, and what should I do next.

It is also a system that works in both attack and defence, meaning the team's shape in possession and out of possession mirrors each other. Players always know what to do and where to go — the game becomes readable.

Phases of play — how the system works

PHASE 1 Set Piece to First Contact

Trigger: Scrum or lineout won.

Attack: The #9 is the base of the diamond. The forward pod (8, 6, 7) drives forward in a tight channel. The #10 holds depth to pivot and direct. The back line (12, 13) aligns flat and wide to threaten the edge immediately.

Objective: Go forward. Win the collision or create a 2v1 off the set piece before the defence can reorganise. The forward pod creates the go-forward platform; the backs read the picture and decide width or carry.

Key trigger: If the forward pod wins the collision and goes past the gain line — backs attack the width immediately. If the collision is held — 9 recycles, 10 resets the shape.

PHASE 2 Multi-Phase Continuity

Trigger: Ball retained after first contact. Ruck secured.

Attack: The 9 picks the fastest ball delivery option. The 10 stands flat or moves closer to act as a second receiver option. 12 and 13 slide to the wider channel. The back three (11, 14, 15) hold width — they are the space exploiters. The forward pod reshapes — one player at the ruck to secure, two reset into the pod ahead of the next carrier.

Objective: Create a numbers advantage — find the 2v1 or 3v2 through the diamond's width before the defence gets set. If the defence blitzes, kick into the space behind. If they drift, attack the short side. Read first, react second.

Key habit: Every player after a ruck must ask — am I the next carrier, the next support, or the width option? The system only works if everyone is moving with purpose.

PHASE 3 The Kicking Game — Zone Decisions

When to kick: The kicking game is the diamond's long-range weapon. Zone-based decision-making applies — not every position kicks, and not every situation calls for a kick.

Own 22Kick for territory. Long kick, high and straight. Relieve pressure — don't try to play.
22–50mKick to contest. Box kick, cross-field, or grubber behind the defensive line. Create a 50/50 contest.
Opp 22Keep the ball. Attack with width. Kicking in the opposition 22 surrenders territory already won.

The back three as a kicking unit: 15 organises the kick receipt. 11 and 14 chase every kick. The kicker (usually 9 or 10) communicates the kick type before executing. No surprise kicks — the whole back three must know the plan.

DEFENCE Defensive Shape — Mirror the Diamond

Principle: The defensive diamond mirrors the attacking diamond. Every player has the same structural responsibility in and out of possession.

Line speed: The defensive line moves up as one unit. 10 calls the line — flat or deep, blitz or drift — based on the picture in front of them. All players respond to the call simultaneously. No individuals — the line is only as fast as its slowest mover.

Blitz: Used when the opposition has limited space and their #9 is slow. All 7 backs step up aggressively on the snap. The #10 calls "BLITZ" before the ball leaves the ruck. The forward pod covers any pick and go from the base.

Drift: Used in open field with wide ball. Each defender marks one man and drifts out. No one man-marks across to the next defender until the carrier has committed their pass. One-out passes are the drift's strength — but wide overlaps are its weakness.

Guard and post: After every ruck, one forward covers the base of the ruck (post) — protecting against the #8 pick-up or scrum-half snipe. The second forward covers one channel out from post (guard). The third forward joins the defensive line outside the posts.

Sub-Units — How We Build the Team

The 1-3-3-1 system is built on five sub-units that work together. Every player belongs to a sub-unit — and every sub-unit has a primary job. Understanding your sub-unit is as important as understanding your individual position.

1, 2, 3, 4, 5 FRONT FIVE

The engine room. The platform builders. Everything starts here.

Primary job

Win the set piece. Go forward. Secure the ruck. Provide the go-forward platform from which the backs launch. If the Front Five does not function, the system does not function.

Key responsibilities

  • Props (1, 3): contested scrum technique, binding, driving. Hip height below shoulder height at all times. Front row must move as one unit.
  • Hooker (2): accurate lineout throw. Hook in scrums. Highly mobile — a modern hooker is a ball-carrying threat around the fringes.
  • Locks (4, 5): scrum engine — normal binding now permitted. Lineout callers and jumpers. The heartbeat of the pack.
  • All five: arrive at every ruck before the defence. Secure the ball, drive through, create the platform. Width is the backs' job — go-forward is yours.
Season development focus: Set piece cohesion. Ruck arrival and security. Mobility — get to the breakdown faster than the opposition.
6, 7, 8 BACK ROW

The connectors. Attack and defend. Bridge the front five and the backs.

Primary job

Drive the forward pod in attack. Contest every breakdown in defence. Cover the field. The back row is the most physically demanding sub-unit — you must be everywhere.

Key responsibilities

  • Number 8: leads the forward pod in attack. Can now pick up from the base of the scrum and run. Primary carrier. Works in close co-operation with 6 and 7 at the breakdown. Apex predator.
  • Flankers (6, 7): defined breakdown roles — one secures the ruck, one looks to jackal or carry. Poaching, disrupting, tackling across the field. At the heart of the pod system.
  • All three: guard/post defensive role at every ruck. Pod organisation in attack. Set piece platform for starter plays.
Season development focus: Ruck compete — jackal technique, legal body position. Pod cohesion — know when to carry, when to support. Communication with 9 at every breakdown.
9, 10 HALF BACKS

The decision-makers. The directors. The game runs through this sub-unit.

Primary job

Control the tempo and shape of play. The #9 is the base of the diamond. The #10 is the pivot — they read the picture and choose the play. Both must be communicating at all times.

Key responsibilities

  • Scrum-half (9): Vocal. Very vocal. Talking all the time. Working in close co-operation with 8 and 10. Sets the tempo. Can now box kick from scrums and lineouts. Pass, pick, run, or kick — but always decide quickly.
  • Fly-half (10): Full portfolio of attacking options. Kick, pass, carry/run. Dictates use of starter plays from set piece. In the 1-3-3-1 the 10 is the pivot — the system's brain. Calling line speed in defence.
  • Both: must read the defensive picture together and communicate the play option before it happens.
Season development focus: Communication — be loud, be clear, be early. Decision speed — the defence moves on the ball, so decisions must be ahead of the defence. Kicking game — when, where, and what type.
12, 13 CENTRES

The midfield engine. Attack the space. Lead the defensive line.

Primary job

In attack: exploit the space between the forward pod and the wide channels. In defence: lead the line, set the tempo, communicate to the wings.

Key responsibilities

  • Inside centre (12): closest to the forward pod. Primary defensive responsibility — must track the crash ball and the 10's run. In attack, the first option off the #10's pass — flat, hard, and fast.
  • Outside centre (13): the edge exploiter. Reads whether the space is inside or outside the last defender. Communicates to the wing and full-back. Line "shooters" is an option — stepping up to hit the ball at pace and create a mismatch.
  • Both: defensive line speed as a weapon. Depth management — knowing when to go flat and when to hold depth. Tie in with players either side.
Season development focus: Midfield penetration — commit the defender, create the overlap. Defensive communication — call the line, don't wait for the 10 to call for you. Support lines either side of strike runners.
11, 14, 15 BACK THREE

The space exploiters. The width. The last line of defence and the first line of counter-attack.

Primary job

In attack: finish and exploit. Hold width. Be available for the wide ball and the kick receipt. In defence: cover the back field. Communicate field position. Operate as a pendulum unit.

Key responsibilities

  • Wings (11, 14): Full-size pitch means far more ground to cover. Kick chase — every kick, sprint hard, contest the ball. Kick return and counter-attack. Defensive red zone kicking as an option. Trail lines in attack — be available after the carry.
  • Full-back (15): the organiser. Receives kicks in a full-size game — high ball, timing, calling. Counter-attack from deep. Kicking out of hand under pressure. Covering kicks behind the line. Links the wings and sets the position of the pendulum.
  • All three: operate as one unit. The pendulum approach — as the ball goes right, the left wing tucks in as cover, the right wing goes wide, the 15 positions to support either side. This sub-unit rotates together.
Season development focus: Kick-receipt technique. The pendulum — practise moving as a unit in training. Counter-attack reads — when to run, when to kick back. Communication — the 15 must be the loudest voice in the back field.

RFU Regulation 15 · 2025–26

Moving Up to U14

The five biggest changes from U13 to U14, and a full rule comparison. Based on RFU Regulation 15, Appendices 7 & 8, effective 1 August 2025.

1

15-a-side

Up from 13-a-side. Two extra players, more space, more positional responsibility for everyone.

2

Full-size pitch

100m × 70m (was 90m × 60m). +50% more ground per player. Fitness matters from day one.

3

8-man scrum

Full 3-4-1 formation. #8 can pick up and run. Scrum-half can now box kick from the base.

4

Lineouts begin

U13 has no lineouts — touch restarts with a free pass. At U14 uncontested lineouts begin. No lifting until U15.

5

Box kicks & drop goals

The full kicking game opens up. Box kicks, drop goals, and conversions are all introduced.

Player welfare

Looking After Yourself

It is critical that players start to understand the role they play in their own welfare. Consistent habits around sleep, nutrition, hydration and recovery are as important as what you do on the pitch.

🌙 Sleep — Your Most Powerful Recovery Tool

Sleep is not rest. Sleep is when your body does its most important work — repairing muscle damage, consolidating the skills you practised, and releasing the hormones that drive growth and development.

🧬
Growth hormone

Up to 70% of the day's growth hormone is released during deep sleep — specifically in the first 2–3 hours of the night. This is the hormone responsible for muscle repair, bone development, and recovery from training. No sleep = no growth hormone = slower recovery and development.

🧠
Skill consolidation

Every drill, passing technique, and movement pattern you practise is consolidated during REM sleep. The neural pathways that make skills feel automatic are built overnight. Players who practise and then sleep properly improve faster than those who practise without adequate sleep.

💪
Muscle repair

Training causes microscopic damage to muscle fibres. Sleep — particularly deep sleep — triggers the repair and rebuilding process that makes muscles stronger. Without 8–9 hours, this process is incomplete. You train but don't adapt.

😤
Mental resilience

Decision-making under pressure (tackle timing, reading the defensive line, calling in attack) deteriorates significantly with poor sleep. A player who slept 6 hours makes worse decisions under pressure than one who slept 9. It's not attitude — it's physiology.

Target: 8–9 hours per night. Non-negotiable throughout the programme. Sleep deficits from the week cannot be recovered at the weekend.

🔥 Warm-Up — Non-Negotiable Before Every Session

Cold muscles tear. Warm muscles bend. A 10-minute warm-up raises muscle temperature, increases blood flow, and prepares tendons and ligaments for explosive loads. At U14, your body is still developing — skipping the warm-up is the single biggest cause of avoidable injuries in junior rugby.

A proper warm-up also improves reaction time, sprint speed, and passing accuracy from the very first minute of the session.

What good looks like (5–10 mins, every session):
  • Leg swings, arm circles, hip rotations
  • Walking knee drives, lateral shuffles
  • A-skips, bounding, change of direction
  • Gentle jog building to strides
  • Thursday sessions use RFU Activate

❄️ Recovery & Cool-Down

Training breaks the body down. Recovery builds it back stronger. Muscles grow, fitness improves, and neural pathways sharpen during rest — not during the session. A warm-down signals the body to switch from performance mode to repair mode.

5 minutes after every session:
  • Easy jog or walk — 2 minutes
  • Hip flexor stretch — 30s each side
  • Quad & hamstring — 30s each
  • Calf & thoracic rotation — 30s each

Eat within 60 minutes of finishing a session — carbohydrates to restore energy, protein to repair muscle. This window matters.

💧 Nutrition & Hydration

The body needs clean lean protein, complex carbohydrates, fibre, and healthy fats to support growth, recovery, and performance.

  • If you feel thirsty, it's already too late — hydrate before training
  • Monitor urine colour as a hydration guide (pale yellow = good)
  • Getting hydration levels right takes time — everyone is different
  • Compound weights are not needed yet — bodyweight, stretching, and mobility are better at this age
  • Limit ultra-processed food and high-sugar drinks during the programme
Food is fuel. Treat it that way.

🛑 Pain vs Effort

There is a real difference between the discomfort of hard effort — lungs burning, legs heavy — and actual pain. If you feel sharp, persistent, or worsening pain:

  • Stop immediately
  • Tell a parent and your coach
  • Do not train through it

Never train on consecutive days if carrying an injury. A week off now beats missing the whole season. Rehabbing an injury correctly is always more important than training through it.

Limit contact sessions to once within any 48-hour period.

👪 For Parents

  • Half-game rule is mandatory — every player plays at least half of available time. Speak to the coach if this is not happening.
  • No lifting at U14 — lineout lifting is only permitted from U15. Mandatory safety rule.
  • Scrums are closely managed — the 1.5m push limit is strictly enforced.
  • Match ends at 50-point difference — RFU rules protect players in mismatched fixtures.
  • Medical info — share any medical condition, injury history or welfare concern with the coaching team before the season starts.
