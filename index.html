How we think about rugby

Coaching Philosophy

Everything we do this season starts here. Before tactics, before fitness, before the system — this is what we believe and how we work.

The four pillars of player development

These four areas guide everything we observe, coach, and measure. They apply equally to every player, every session, every game.

1

Game Understanding

Reading the game — knowing where to be, why you're there, and what happens next. Shape in attack and defence. Knowing your role in the system. Making correct decisions under pressure. What's my next job? is the question every player should be asking constantly.

2

Outstanding Behaviours & Coachability

Attitude, effort, and communication on the pitch. How a player carries themselves in a team environment. Being receptive to coaching — not just accepting feedback but actively seeking it. The player who improves fastest is rarely the most talented one. It's the one who listens, reflects, and applies.

3

Athletic Potential

Speed, endurance, power, agility, and movement quality. Not just current fitness — but the potential and willingness to develop physically through consistent, disciplined work. The summer programme is built specifically around this. What you do between now and September will determine where you start the season.

4

Fundamental Skills

Catch & Pass, Carry, Tackle, and Breakdown. The four core skills that underpin everything else. No system, no tactic, no formation works if these aren't being executed reliably under pressure. We will return to these foundations constantly throughout the season, regardless of what else is being developed.

🎯

What We're Building

  • Player-led goals — players own their development and set their own targets. We facilitate, we don't prescribe.
  • Player-led consistency — habits built in training transfer to matchday automatically. You play how you train.
  • Team developmental goals — on and off the field, we grow together. Individual progress serves the team.
  • Whole-player development — sleep, nutrition, hydration and mindset matter as much as what happens on the pitch.
  • Coach as facilitator — we channel messages through on-field leaders, not around them. The captain and senior players lead. We create the conditions for that to happen.
🧭

How We Coach

  • Observe behaviours, not just results. How a player responds to adversity tells us more than the scoreboard.
  • Support by responding appropriately — not reacting emotionally first. Considered coaching, not knee-jerk criticism.
  • Decisions made before game day — where possible, no player should be making a decision for the first time on the pitch. We prepare decisions in training.
  • Quiet, slow, deliberate in walk-throughs and pre-game environments. Lower the noise, raise the attention.
  • Let players talk and lead. Our job is to create the conditions for that to happen — not to fill every silence.
🔄

Why This Season Is Different

The U14 season is a deliberate transition year — an RFU "by design" pathway bridging children's rugby and the junior game. Laws, pitch size and team shape all move closer to the adult version simultaneously. Structure, space, set piece, and strategy all grow in importance at once. Our job is to make that step feel manageable, exciting, and grounded in good habits.

Winchester does not develop teams built around massive units who bulldoze through the opposition. At the underage grades, we produce intelligent, athletic teams who play smart rugby — knowing when to apply structure and when to exploit the space that structure exposes in the opposition's defence. Likewise in defence, defending as a team and using it as a weapon has always been the hallmark of our older age grade teams when they have proven ready for the step up.

This season we need to evolve from 15 individuals playing a team sport into a cohesive unit that can play intelligent, heads-up rugby. This is the year where we will start to see moments coming together in games — moments where all of the above clicks. Over the course of the season, the extent to which our game contains those moments will be down to how we teach, develop and embrace these philosophies together.

Our playing approach

The 1-3-3-1 System & Diamond Formation

The 1-3-3-1 is a forward distribution framework — it describes how the eight forwards spread intelligently across the pitch width between breakdowns. This is the foundation of everything we do in attack and defence. Learn the framework, then the rest of the game becomes readable.

What is the 1-3-3-1?

Why the 1-3-3-1 works

🛡️

Protection in midfield

Two pods of three forwards where defenders are most numerous means the attack always has bodies around the ball. Quick, clean rucks, quick ball to the backs. The defence cannot flood the midfield without leaving the edges open.

👁️

Decoy and misdirection

The midfield pods draw defensive attention inward. When defenders commit to stopping the pod carry, the ball is moved wide to the lone forward or the backs with space to run into. The pods do not need to score — they need to pull defenders.

⚖️

Symmetry — attack both sides

Because the formation is identical left and right, the team can switch attack direction instantly without reorganising. The defence must defend the full width. No strong side, no weak side — just space to be found and exploited.

How it works — phase by phase

The 1-3-3-1 is not a set piece move. It is a framework that must reform and function across every phase of play. This is the hardest part to learn — and the most important.

SET PIECE Establishing the shape from scrum or lineout

The starting picture: From a scrum or lineout, the 1-3-3-1 shape should be set before the ball is won. The two wide forwards (the "1s") position themselves near the 15-metre lines. The two pods of three arrange themselves either side of the breakdown point. The backs — 9, 10, centres, wings — connect the pods and provide width beyond the wide forwards.

From a scrum: The No.8 leads the first pod carry from the base or passes to the 9. The first pod of three attacks the channel with the most defenders. The 9 connects — deciding whether to feed the pod, pass to the 10, or go themselves. The second pod holds their position on the opposite side, ready for the next phase.

From a lineout: The ball is secured and passed to the 9. The first pod attacks the gain line. The second pod begins moving to the other side of the breakdown to reform for phase two. The wide forward on the ball side holds their width — they are the indicator of available space on the edge.

Critical habit: The shape does not form at the set piece. It reforms at the second or third phase. In the early weeks, expect the formation to break down in phase two — this is normal. The goal is to make the reset automatic over time.

PHASE PLAY The pods in action — carry, pass, and reload

How the pods work: Each pod of three operates as a unit. The lead player in the pod takes the carry and presents the ball. One of the other two secures the ruck. The third resets — either back into their pod position for the next phase, or they swap with a back to create a mismatch. The pod does not need to score a try. Its job is to go past the gain line, secure a fast ruck, and draw defenders inward.

The decoy pod: While the first pod carries, the second pod positions itself on the opposite side of the breakdown, hands up, running convincing lines. Their job is to occupy defenders without the ball. If three defenders watch the second pod and they don't have the ball, three defenders have been removed from where the ball is going. That is the system working.

The wide forward: The lone forward in the wide channel holds their position outside the 15-metre line. They provide the link between the pods and the backs. When the ball moves to their side, they become the first receiver option for a short carry that holds a defensive winger in. They must be athletic enough to carry, pass, and get back into their channel quickly.

The backs' role: The 9 connects every phase. The 10 and 12 marshal the pods from behind, reading when the defence commits inward and when to release the ball wide. The wings stay wide — wide backs are the reward for a well-functioning pod system. If the pods are working, a wing will eventually receive the ball with one defender to beat.

SWITCHING SIDES Using symmetry to change the point of attack

The switch: One of the 1-3-3-1's most powerful attributes is the ability to switch the attack from one side to the other without losing shape. After the first pod has carried and a ruck is secured, the 9 or 10 can move the ball back across to the second pod — which has spent two phases drawing defenders away from the ball. That second pod now attacks a defence that has been pulled to one side.

What the defence sees: The defence has been chasing the first pod for two phases. They have drifted toward the ball side. When the ball is switched, the second pod is attacking a disorganised defensive line with defenders still recovering position. The gain line becomes easier to breach. This is why patience across phases is the system's most important discipline.

The trigger: The 10 or 12 marshalling the second pod is responsible for calling the switch. When they see the defence has over-committed to the first pod's side — typically after two or three phases through the first pod — they call for the ball and release the second pod. The 9 must be listening for this call constantly.

Common mistake: Switching too early. If the defence has not yet committed to the first pod's side, a switch simply finds an equally organised defensive line on the other side. The switch works because of patience, not despite it. Build the phases first.

ATTACK THE EDGE When to release the backs and finish wide

The moment: When the pods have drawn enough defenders inward, the wide forward and backs have a numerical advantage on the edge. The 10 or 12 reads this moment and releases the ball wide — behind the pods, past the wide forward, and into the hands of the centre or wing with space to run.

The tunnel pass: A key skill in the 1-3-3-1 is the pass that goes behind the pod — appearing to threaten the pod carry, then delivering the ball outside them to the backs. The defender who has committed to stopping the pod carry is now out of the play. The 10 and 12 must develop this pass specifically.

What the backs need: The wings must hold their width throughout — never drifting infield during the pod phases. When the ball is released, the wing must be in position to run onto it at pace. A wing who has drifted infield during the phase play will be in traffic when the ball arrives. Width is discipline, not instinct.

The wide forward's final role: As the ball moves out to the backs, the wide forward peels inward to provide a short carry option if the outside defence has held. They must read whether the edge is truly clear before the backs commit. If it is not clear, the wide forward's carry protects the ball and resets the shape for another attack.

DEFENCE How the 1-3-3-1 informs defensive structure

The defensive principle: The 1-3-3-1 attacking framework has a defensive counterpart. The same forward distribution that creates attacking width also provides a structured defensive reset. When the team loses the ball, the forwards are already spread — they do not all need to chase back from a central ruck position.

Guard and post: At every ruck in defence, one forward covers post (directly behind the 9, protecting against the snipe or pick-and-go) and one covers guard (one channel wider). The remaining forwards spread to the defensive channels that mirror their attacking positions. The 9 organises this from the ruck base.

Line speed: The 10 calls the defensive line — flat and aggressive (blitz) or disciplined and drifting. This call is made before the opposition's 9 touches the ball, not after. The whole back line responds to one call. The pods provide the defensive cover for any crash ball between the 10 and the centres.

The lone forward in defence: The wide forward in defence covers the channel between the last centre and the winger. This is the most dangerous space in midfield defence — the area where a quick switch from pods to edge can create a 2v1. The wide forward must not drift to the ruck; their job is to be in this channel before the ball arrives there.

◆

Coming next season — the diamond pods

Once players have internalised the 1-3-3-1 framework — knowing their channel, reading the pods, understanding the switch — the next evolution is to develop each pod of three into its own mini diamond. Each pod operates with an apex player, two carriers, and an organiser, mirroring the team's overall attacking shape at a smaller scale. This allows the pod to attack in multiple directions, not just straight, and creates far more decision-making complexity for the defence. This development will begin when the coaching team determines the squad is ready.

Moving Up to U14

The five biggest changes and a full rule comparison. Based on RFU Regulation 15 effective 1 August 2025.

1

15-a-side

Up from 13-a-side. More space, more positional responsibility for everyone.

2

Full-size pitch

100m × 70m. Roughly 50% more ground per player. Fitness matters from day one.

3

8-man scrum

Full 3-4-1 formation. #8 can pick up and run. Scrum-half can box kick from the base.

4

Lineouts begin

No lineouts at U13 — free pass from touch. At U14 uncontested lineouts begin. No lifting until U15.

5

Full kicking game

Box kicks, drop goals, and conversions all introduced at U14 for the first time.

Player welfare

Looking After Yourself

Consistent habits around sleep, nutrition, hydration and recovery are as important as what you do on the pitch. This section is a guide for players — and a reference for parents.

🌙

Sleep — Your Most Powerful Recovery Tool

8–9 hours per night. Non-negotiable. This is not optional advice.

Sleep is not rest. Sleep is when your body does its most important work — repairing muscle, consolidating skills, and releasing the hormones that drive development. It is the single most impactful thing a young athlete can do for their performance, and it costs nothing.

🧬
Growth hormone

Up to 70% of daily growth hormone is released during deep sleep — specifically in the first 2–3 hours of the night. Growth hormone drives muscle repair, bone development, and recovery. No deep sleep = severely reduced growth hormone = slower physical development and longer recovery between sessions.

🧠
Skill consolidation

Every drill, passing technique, and movement pattern you practise is consolidated during REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep. The neural pathways that make skills feel automatic are literally built overnight. Players who train and sleep properly improve measurably faster than those who train without adequate sleep.

💪
Muscle repair

Training causes microscopic damage to muscle fibres. This is normal and necessary — but the repair and rebuilding that makes muscles stronger happens during deep sleep. Without sufficient sleep, this process is incomplete. You train hard, but your body cannot adapt. The training is essentially wasted.

🎯
Decision-making

Decision-making under pressure — tackle timing, reading the defensive line, calling in attack — deteriorates significantly with sleep deprivation. Research shows that after 17–19 hours awake, cognitive performance is equivalent to a blood alcohol level of 0.05%. A player who slept 6 hours will make worse decisions under match pressure. It is not attitude. It is physiology.

Target: 8–9 hours every night throughout the programme. Sleep debt from the week cannot be recovered at the weekend — consistency matters more than quantity on any single night.
💧

Hydration — Start Before You're Thirsty

If you feel thirsty during a session, you are already 1–2% dehydrated. Performance drops from that point.

Water makes up approximately 60% of body weight. Even mild dehydration of 1–2% reduces aerobic capacity, sprint performance, and cognitive function. For a young rugby player, that means slower, less explosive, and making worse decisions — all at once.

Before training

Drink 400–600ml of water in the 2 hours before a session. Arrive hydrated — not trying to catch up during the session.

During training

Sip 150–250ml every 15–20 minutes. Don't wait until a water break — drink at every opportunity during natural pauses in play.

After training

Drink 500ml–1 litre within 30 minutes of finishing. Continue drinking regularly throughout the rest of the day.

How to check

Urine colour is the simplest guide. Pale straw yellow = well hydrated. Dark yellow = dehydrated. Clear = possibly over-hydrated (electrolytes may be low). Aim for pale straw consistently.

Hot weather

In warm weather, sweat rates increase significantly. Add electrolytes (a pinch of salt in water, or a sports drink) for sessions longer than 60 minutes. Water alone does not replace the sodium lost through sweat.

Establish a hydration habit daily — not just on training days. Players who are chronically under-hydrated between sessions will never perform at their best on the pitch.
🥗

Nutrition — Fuelling Development

You cannot out-train a poor diet. Nutrition is the foundation that makes everything else work.

At 13–14, the body is undergoing significant development. Training load is increasing. The demand on the body is genuinely high. Getting nutrition right doesn't mean following a complex plan — it means understanding the basics and applying them consistently.

The four macronutrients and what they do

⚡
Carbohydrates

Primary energy source for exercise

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel for high-intensity work — sprinting, tackling, explosive movements. Without adequate carbs before training, the body runs out of available energy quickly, leading to fatigue and reduced performance.

Good sources: Wholegrain bread, rice, pasta, oats, potatoes, fruit.

Pre-exercise: eat 2–3 hours before. Post-exercise: replenish within 30–60 mins.
🔧
Protein

Repairs and builds muscle

Protein provides the building blocks (amino acids) that repair muscle damaged during training and build new, stronger muscle fibres. At this age, 1.2–1.6g of protein per kg of body weight per day is appropriate — a 55kg player needs roughly 66–88g per day.

Good sources: Chicken, fish, eggs, lean beef, dairy (milk, yoghurt), legumes (lentils, chickpeas), nuts.

Key window: 20–40g of protein within 30 minutes of finishing a session accelerates recovery significantly.
🛡️
Healthy Fats

Hormone production, joint health, brain function

Fat is essential for hormone production (including testosterone and growth hormone), absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), maintaining joint health, and brain function. Don't avoid fat — avoid the wrong types of fat.

Good sources: Oily fish (salmon, mackerel), avocado, olive oil, nuts and seeds, eggs.

Avoid: Trans fats found in heavily processed food. These actively interfere with hormone function.
🌱
Fibre

Gut health, absorption, sustained energy

Fibre slows digestion, which means energy from food is released more steadily rather than in spikes and crashes. Good gut health also improves the absorption of all other nutrients — a poorly functioning gut means less benefit from even a good diet.

Good sources: Vegetables, fruit (especially with skin), wholegrains, oats, legumes.

Target: Aim for 5 portions of fruit and vegetables per day as a starting point.

Pre and post-exercise nutrition

2–3 hours before training

A balanced meal with moderate carbohydrates, some protein, and low fat and fibre. The body needs time to digest before high-intensity work.

Examples: Chicken and rice · pasta with lean protein · jacket potato with tuna · porridge with banana

→
30–60 mins before / during

A small, easily digestible carbohydrate snack if needed. Avoid anything high in fat or fibre close to training — slow to digest and will cause discomfort.

Examples: Banana · rice cakes · small handful of dried fruit

→
Within 30–60 mins after

The most important nutritional window. Replenish glycogen (carbs) and begin muscle repair (protein). The body's ability to absorb and use these nutrients is highest immediately after exercise.

Examples: Chocolate milk · chicken wrap · eggs on toast · Greek yoghurt with fruit

Why ultra-processed food undermines recovery

Ultra-processed foods (UPFs) — crisps, sweets, fast food, fizzy drinks, packaged snacks, most breakfast cereals — are engineered to be consumed quickly and in large amounts. They are typically high in refined sugar, refined flour, salt, and trans fats, and low in fibre, vitamins, and minerals.

They spike and crash blood sugar

Refined sugar and carbohydrates cause a rapid blood sugar spike followed by a sharp crash. After the crash, energy is low, focus drops, and the body signals hunger again. For a rugby player, this means inconsistent energy levels — performing well in the first 20 minutes and fading thereafter.

They interfere with recovery

High levels of refined ingredients trigger an inflammatory response in the body. This is the opposite of what recovery requires. Inflammation is the enemy of repair — it slows muscle recovery, increases soreness, and disrupts sleep quality. Poor sleep quality means less growth hormone released, completing the negative cycle.

They displace nutrition

Every time a player chooses a UPF, they are filling space that could have been used by something that actually supports recovery. It's not just about what UPFs do — it's about what they prevent. A bag of crisps eaten after training doesn't just fail to help; it actively crowds out the protein and micronutrients that would have repaired muscle tissue.

They affect mood and cognition

Diets high in UPFs are consistently associated with higher rates of anxiety, poor concentration, and low mood in adolescents. Good decision-making under pressure — reading the defensive line, calling in attack — requires a well-nourished brain. UPFs do not provide the omega-3 fatty acids, B vitamins, and minerals the brain needs to function under stress.

The goal is not perfection. It's consistency. Make the better choice 80% of the time and the other 20% won't matter.
🤸

Stretching & Mobility — The Underrated Foundation

Most injuries in junior rugby are caused by inadequate preparation or recovery. Stretching and mobility address both.

At 13–14, players are growing rapidly. Bones grow faster than muscles — which means muscles are under constant tension during growth spurts. This is why Osgood-Schlatter disease (knee pain), hamstring tightness, and hip flexor issues are so common at this age. Targeted stretching and mobility work directly addresses this.

Types of stretching — when to use each

🔄
Dynamic stretching
Use: Before training and matches

Controlled movements that take joints through their full range of motion — leg swings, arm circles, hip rotations, walking lunges, high knees. Raises muscle temperature, improves neural activation, and prepares the body for explosive movements. This is your pre-session warm-up.

  • Leg swings (front/back and lateral) — 10 each direction
  • Hip circles — 10 each direction
  • Walking knee drives — length of pitch
  • Lateral shuffles with arm swings — length of pitch
  • A-skips and bounding — length of pitch
⏸️
Static stretching
Use: After training and matches only

Holding a stretch position for 20–40 seconds. Lengthens muscle, improves flexibility over time, and signals the body to switch from performance mode to recovery mode. Never use static stretching as a warm-up — it temporarily reduces muscle power output.

  • Hip flexor lunge stretch — 30s each side
  • Standing quad stretch — 30s each side
  • Seated hamstring stretch — 30s each side
  • Calf stretch against a wall — 30s each
  • Chest and shoulder opener — 30s
  • Thoracic rotation (seated) — 10 reps each side
🎯
Mobility work
Use: Daily — even on rest days

Controlled movement through a joint's full active range — not just stretching a muscle but training the nervous system to control that range. At 13–14 this is especially important because rapid growth can limit range of motion even in players who feel flexible.

  • 90/90 hip mobility — 2 mins per side
  • Thoracic spine rotation on all fours — 10 reps
  • Deep squat hold — build to 60s
  • Ankle circles and dorsiflexion drill — 30s each
  • Shoulder CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations) — 5 slow reps

Priority areas for U14 rugby players

Hip flexors
Tight from prolonged sitting (school). Limit stride length and tackle drive. Most common source of lower back pain in young athletes. Stretch daily.
Hamstrings
Most frequently strained muscle in rugby. Tighten rapidly during growth spurts. Adequate hamstring flexibility is the most reliable predictor of avoiding sprint-related injury.
Thoracic spine
Stiffness here limits scrum technique, passing range, and tackle position. Poor thoracic mobility is compensated by lumbar spine — which leads to lower back injuries over time.
Ankles
Limited ankle dorsiflexion (the ability to flex the ankle) is the most underappreciated performance limiter in rugby. It affects sprint mechanics, tackle position, and scrum drive.
Shoulders
Tackle technique and lineout work both demand shoulder stability and range. Tight shoulders also contribute to neck and upper back issues — especially in front row players.
10 minutes of deliberate mobility work daily — even on rest days — will have a greater long-term impact on a player's development than an extra hour of training without it.

🔥 Warm-Up — Non-Negotiable

Cold muscles tear. Warm muscles bend. A 10-minute warm-up raises muscle temperature, increases blood flow, and prepares tendons and ligaments for explosive loads. At U14, your body is still developing — skipping the warm-up is the single biggest cause of avoidable injuries.

Thursday sessions use the RFU Activate programme from Phase 3 — 15 minutes of structured warm-up specifically designed for this age group.

If you're late to a session, don't skip the warm-up to catch up. Warm up first, join the session second.

❄️ Recovery & Cool-Down

Training breaks the body down. Recovery builds it back stronger. A cool-down signals the body to switch from performance to repair mode. 5 minutes after every session.

  • Easy jog or walk — 2 minutes
  • Hip flexor stretch — 30s each side
  • Quad and hamstring — 30s each
  • Calf and thoracic rotation — 30s each

Eat within 30–60 minutes of finishing. The recovery window is real — use it.

👪
